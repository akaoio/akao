# Decentralized-First Design

Decentralization in this project is not a marketing adjective. It is a design bias: assume central control is fragile, expensive, or undesirable, and then ask how much of the system can survive without it.

## Main convictions

- Users should not need to surrender custody in order to participate.
- The participant should be treated as an active agent, not merely a dependent consumer.
- Sync and coordination should emerge from protocols, not from mandatory central servers.
- Platforms should arbitrate as little as possible and possess as little power as possible.

## What this means in practice

The question is not whether the center can do the job. Usually it can. The question is whether the center should own the job. A decentralized-first stance asks, again and again, whether power can be pushed outward without destroying coherence.

## Why this is hard

Decentralized systems are more honest about real-world complexity. Coordination, delay, inconsistency, abuse, and recovery do not disappear; they simply stop being hidden behind a central promise.

That cost is accepted because sovereignty is treated as a real good, not a decorative extra.

## The deeper principle

Power should move to the edges whenever the system can still remain intelligible and secure.

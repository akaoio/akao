# Cryptographic Sovereignty

Identity, trust, and authority are strongest when they rely less on institutional memory and more on verifiable mathematics.

## Main convictions

- Authentication should minimize shared secrets and custodial recovery paths.
- Identity should be anchored in cryptographic proof, not in weak social mechanisms.
- Users should retain maximal control over keys, wallets, and derived secrets.
- Trust should be narrowed to explicit assumptions rather than vague platform promises.

## Why passkeys matter

The attraction of stronger cryptographic identity is not novelty. It is moral economy. A system should ask to hold as little of the user as possible. The less it possesses, the less it can lose, exploit, or betray.

## Why this expands beyond login

This idea extends beyond login. Whenever authority can be proven rather than merely claimed, dependency on institutional trust can shrink.

## The deeper principle

Real sovereignty means the system can verify you without possessing you.
